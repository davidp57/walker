# Walker

Walker is a personal time tracker. You use it during the day to record what you're working on, and
at the end of each pay period it hands you a ready-to-copy view of your time, shaped to match your
company's timesheet system.

## The problem it solves

Most corporate timesheet systems are painful to use directly: clunky grids, dropdown-driven charge
codes, and a strict schedule for when entries are due. Nobody wants to reconstruct two weeks of work
from memory on the last day of the period.

Walker sits between your actual work and that system. During the day you start a timer with one
click — no forms, no categorizing up front — and describe or classify the entry whenever it's
convenient, even after the fact. When the period ends, Walker shows you a grid that already looks like
your timesheet system's own entry screen, pre-totaled and organized by charge code, so re-typing it
there is fast and low-risk.

## What Walker is not

- It does not talk to your timesheet system over the network, log into it, or submit anything on your
  behalf. You still do the final data entry yourself — Walker just makes that data entry mechanical
  instead of a memory exercise.
- It does not round your time or chase a daily/weekly hour target. It records what you actually did,
  to the minute; rounding to whatever increment your timesheet system expects happens when you key it
  in there.
